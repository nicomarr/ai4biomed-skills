# Observational study protocol guidance reference

> **How to use this file.** Load this file during Phase C (framework checks) when the package contains a protocol for an **observational** study (cohort / case-control / cross-sectional / registry / surveillance / qualitative). SPIRIT 2025 does not apply to observational research; this reference plays the analogous role of supplying a checklist of items reviewers should verify in an observational protocol. The primary primary-source authority is **STROBE** (Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology, 2007), which is a *reporting* guideline for completed studies; it is applied here as a forward-looking guide. Reviewers should be told this caveat explicitly when findings are based on STROBE — the items are useful prompts but they do not have the protocol-specific authority that SPIRIT carries for trials.

Domain-extended STROBE variants exist (STROBE-MR for Mendelian randomisation; STREGA for genetic association studies; RECORD for routinely-collected health data; RECORD-PE for pharmacoepidemiology) — note these where the protocol falls in their domain.

## What STROBE is and is not

STROBE is a 22-item reporting checklist for completed observational studies (cohort, case-control, cross-sectional). It does not address protocol-specific items (timeline, statistical analysis plan, monitoring) the way SPIRIT does for trials. When applied to a protocol, STROBE items act as a *forward-looking guide*: they tell the reviewer which items the study will need to report on later, and therefore which items the protocol should already specify so they can be reported correctly.

Reviewer guidance: when a finding is based on a STROBE item, the source citation should make this explicit — "STROBE 2007 item N (a *reporting* guideline applied here as a forward-looking guide for protocol design)" — so the reviewer can apply appropriate weight.

## STROBE items relevant to protocols (verbatim from the 2007 statement)

The full STROBE 2007 checklist has 22 items. Items 1–10 and 21–22 are most relevant to protocols (items 11–20 are mostly about reported results and discussion, which a protocol does not contain).

| # | Item | Verbatim wording (STROBE 2007) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Title and abstract | "(a) Indicate the study's design with a commonly used term in the title or the abstract. (b) Provide in the abstract an informative and balanced summary of what was done and what was found." (For a protocol: only (a) applies — the title/short title should make the design explicit, e.g., "prospective cohort", "case-control", "cross-sectional".) |
| 2 | Background/rationale | "Explain the scientific background and rationale for the investigation being reported." |
| 3 | Objectives | "State specific objectives, including any prespecified hypotheses." |
| 4 | Study design | "Present key elements of study design early in the paper." |
| 5 | Setting | "Describe the setting, locations, and relevant dates, including periods of recruitment, exposure, follow-up, and data collection." |
| 6 | Participants | "(a) Cohort study—Give the eligibility criteria, and the sources and methods of selection of participants. Describe methods of follow-up. (b) Case-control study—Give the eligibility criteria, and the sources and methods of case ascertainment and control selection. Give the rationale for the choice of cases and controls. (c) Cross-sectional study—Give the eligibility criteria, and the sources and methods of selection of participants. (d) Cohort study—For matched studies, give matching criteria and number of exposed and unexposed. Case-control study—For matched studies, give matching criteria and the number of controls per case." |
| 7 | Variables | "Clearly define all outcomes, exposures, predictors, potential confounders, and effect modifiers. Give diagnostic criteria, if applicable." |
| 8 | Data sources / measurement | "For each variable of interest, give sources of data and details of methods of assessment (measurement). Describe comparability of assessment methods if there is more than one group." |
| 9 | Bias | "Describe any efforts to address potential sources of bias." |
| 10 | Study size | "Explain how the study size was arrived at." |
| 11 | Quantitative variables | "Explain how quantitative variables were handled in the analyses. If applicable, describe which groupings were chosen and why." |
| 12 | Statistical methods | "(a) Describe all statistical methods, including those used to control for confounding. (b) Describe any methods used to examine subgroups and interactions. (c) Explain how missing data were addressed. (d) Cohort study—If applicable, explain how loss to follow-up was addressed. Case-control study—If applicable, explain how matching of cases and controls was addressed. Cross-sectional study—If applicable, describe analytical methods taking account of sampling strategy. (e) Describe any sensitivity analyses." |
| 21 | Generalisability | "Discuss the generalisability (external validity) of the study results." (For a protocol: the protocol should describe the *population to which results are intended to generalise* and any planned external-validity considerations.) |
| 22 | Funding | "Give the source of funding and the role of the funders for the present study and, if applicable, for the original study on which the present article is based." |

## Structural elements reviewers commonly look for in an observational protocol

In addition to the STROBE items above, reviewers typically expect the following structural elements in an observational protocol. These are *not* from a single primary source — they are widely-recognised norms in the observational-research methodology literature and in IRB/REC review practice. Cite as "common observational-protocol structural element" rather than as a primary-source requirement.

1. **Title page** with study identifier, version number and date, PI and co-investigators, sites, sponsor.
2. **Summary / abstract** of the study.
3. **Background and rationale** (STROBE item 2).
4. **Specific objectives and hypotheses** (STROBE item 3) — primary, secondary, exploratory.
5. **Study design** (STROBE item 4) — explicit named design (cohort, case-control, cross-sectional, registry, surveillance, qualitative); rationale for the design choice; comparator group(s) where applicable.
6. **Setting** (STROBE item 5) — sites, geographic scope, dates, populations.
7. **Participants and selection** (STROBE item 6) — inclusion/exclusion criteria; recruitment plan; consent procedures by group; matching strategy if applicable.
8. **Variables — exposures, outcomes, confounders** (STROBE items 7, 8) — measurement instruments, instruments validated/adapted, calibration procedures, blinding of assessors where applicable.
9. **Sample size and power** (STROBE item 10) — explicit calculation or rationale; assumptions stated; expected attrition.
10. **Data collection plan** — instruments, schedule, who collects, training, quality assurance.
11. **Data management and security** — coding, anonymisation, storage location, access controls, retention period, destruction plan. For studies in regulated environments, should reference applicable national-level data-protection law (e.g., GDPR, HIPAA where applicable, national equivalents).
12. **Statistical analysis plan** (STROBE item 12) — primary and secondary analyses; handling of missing data; loss-to-follow-up procedures (cohort) or matching methods (case-control); sensitivity analyses.
13. **Bias mitigation** (STROBE item 9) — selection bias, information bias, recall bias, confounding; explicit mitigation strategies.
14. **Ethical considerations** — approvals required, consent processes by group, vulnerable-population provisions, conflicts of interest, payment for participation.
15. **Timeline** — study activities by phase.
16. **Dissemination plan** — publications, conferences, return of findings to participants and communities.
17. **References**.
18. **Sponsor and funder information** (STROBE item 22) — including the funder's role in the study (or absence of role).
19. **Investigator statement of compliance**.

## Biospecimen-specific considerations for observational studies

Observational studies that collect biological samples (blood, tissue, saliva, buccal swabs, stored specimens, etc.) raise additional protocol items beyond STROBE. The reviewer should check:

- **Sample collection procedures** — volume, timing, who collects, processing window, storage temperature, chain of custody.
- **Sample storage** — primary repository, location, access controls, retention period, destruction or transfer procedure at end of study.
- **Future-use plan** — explicit statement of which future research uses are pre-authorised, which require fresh consent, and which are excluded. Cross-reference WHO ERC sample-storage consent template (see `icf-guidance.md` §3.1A.2).
- **Identifier linkage** — whether the sample remains linked to participant identifiers; researchers' obligations to inform participants of clinically-relevant findings if linked; the choice between linked and de-identified storage and how it is offered to the participant.
- **Sample sharing** — with whom; under what governance; cross-border transfer if applicable; commercial vs non-commercial use restrictions.
- **Sample destruction** — when and how the sample is destroyed; participant's right to request destruction.

For genetic-research-specific considerations, also check:

- **Return-of-results policy** — whether incidental findings are returned to participants; which categories of findings; the return process (clinical genetic counselling, etc.).
- **Commercial profit disclosure** — 45 CFR §46.116(c)(7) requires verbatim *"A statement that the subject's biospecimens (even if identifiers are removed) may be used for commercial profit and whether the subject will or will not share in this commercial profit."*
- **Whole-genome sequencing disclosure** — 45 CFR §46.116(c)(9) requires verbatim *"For research involving biospecimens, whether the research will (if known) or might include whole genome sequencing (i.e., sequencing of a human germline or somatic specimen with the intent to generate the genome or exome sequence of that specimen)."*
- **Data sharing** — central biobank or genomic-data repository deposition (e.g., dbGaP, EGA); data-access committee processes.
- **STREGA** (STrengthening the REporting of Genetic Association studies) — domain-extended STROBE checklist for genetic association studies. Cite as: Little J, Higgins JPT, Ioannidis JPA, et al. *Strengthening the reporting of genetic association studies (STREGA): an extended statement.* PLoS Med 2009;6(2):e1000022. DOI 10.1371/journal.pmed.1000022. Open access.

## Family-based study design considerations

Some observational studies (e.g., genetic-susceptibility studies) recruit related individuals across multiple subject groups. The reviewer should check:

- **Subject group definitions** — explicit listing of all groups with eligibility per group.
- **Within-family consent procedures** — independent consent for each adult family member; parental consent + age-appropriate paediatric assent for minors.
- **Confidentiality of within-family information** — whether information disclosed by one family member can be shared with another; how this is handled in the ICF.
- **Genetic findings affecting relatives** — whether genetic findings discovered in one participant that may affect untested relatives are disclosed, and to whom.

## Qualitative research considerations

If the observational study is qualitative (interviews, focus groups, participant observation, ethnographic), the reviewer should also check:

- **Data type** — audio/video recordings, transcripts, field notes; storage, transcription, and destruction procedures.
- **Confidentiality limits in focus groups** — verbatim from WHO ERC qualitative-studies template: *"The following applies to focus groups…"* — the researcher cannot guarantee that other participants will keep the discussion confidential. The ICF must disclose this.
- **Sensitive topics** — explicit handling of distress that may emerge during interviews; referral procedures.
- **Member-checking** — whether transcripts will be shared with participants for review.
- **Reporting standards** — relevant qualitative-research reporting guidelines (e.g., COREQ for interviews and focus groups: Tong A, Sainsbury P, Craig J. *Consolidated criteria for reporting qualitative research (COREQ)*. Int J Qual Health Care 2007;19(6):349–357. DOI 10.1093/intqhc/mzm042). Treat as forward-looking guidance for what the protocol should specify.

## LMIC and resource-limited setting considerations

These overlap with the LMIC section of `icf-guidance.md` §5; the same considerations apply to the protocol itself.

- **Community engagement** plan documented.
- **Translation and back-translation** of recruitment materials, consent forms, and data-collection instruments.
- **Cultural appropriateness** of measurement instruments — validated locally where possible.
- **Vulnerable populations** — pregnant women, children, adults with limited capacity, refugees, prisoners — explicit justification and protections.
- **Local capacity-building** — collaborations with local institutions; data-sharing terms that respect local sovereignty.
- **Regulatory authority of record** — applicable national research-ethics committee and (for biospecimen export) cross-border transfer authorities.

- All STROBE 2007 items are reproduced verbatim from the open-access publication (PMID 17938396).
- The "structural elements reviewers commonly look for" list is **not from a single primary source** — it is a compilation of widely-recognised observational-research-methodology norms. Findings citing this section should be flagged as drawn from "common observational-protocol practice" rather than from a single named primary source.
- STREGA and COREQ are cited where applicable as domain-extended guidelines.
