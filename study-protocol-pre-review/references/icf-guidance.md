## 2. Required elements — US Common Rule (45 CFR 46.116)

Verbatim from eCFR (current, post-2018 Revised Common Rule), retrieved via Cornell Legal Information Institute primary mirror.

### 2.1 General provisions — 45 CFR 46.116(a)

The general provisions at §46.116(a)(1)–(6) require legally effective informed consent, sufficient opportunity to consider participation, language understandable to the subject, the *reasonable-person* standard, the *concise key-information* presentation (new in 2018), and a prohibition on exculpatory language. The most frequently litigated and reviewer-relevant subparagraph is:

- **§46.116(a)(5)(i) — Key Information requirement (NEW under the Revised Common Rule, effective 21 January 2019).** Verbatim: *"Informed consent must begin with a concise and focused presentation of the key information that is most likely to assist a prospective subject or legally authorized representative in understanding the reasons why one might or might not want to participate in the research. This part of the informed consent must be organized and presented in a way that facilitates comprehension."*
- **§46.116(a)(5)(ii) — Comprehension structure (also NEW in 2018).** Verbatim: *"Informed consent as a whole must present information in sufficient detail relating to the research, and must be organized and presented in a way that does not merely provide lists of isolated facts, but rather facilitates the prospective subject's or legally authorized representative's understanding of the reasons why one might or might not want to participate."*
- **§46.116(a)(4) — Reasonable-person standard (NEW in 2018).** Verbatim: *"The prospective subject or the legally authorized representative must be provided with the information that a reasonable person would want to have in order to make an informed decision about whether to participate, and an opportunity to discuss that information."*

### 2.2 Basic elements — 45 CFR 46.116(b)

Each subject (or LAR) must be provided each of the following nine basic elements (verbatim):

| § | Verbatim text |
|---|---|
| (b)(1) | "A statement that the study involves research, an explanation of the purposes of the research and the expected duration of the subject's participation, a description of the procedures to be followed, and identification of any procedures that are experimental;" |
| (b)(2) | "A description of any reasonably foreseeable risks or discomforts to the subject;" |
| (b)(3) | "A description of any benefits to the subject or to others that may reasonably be expected from the research;" |
| (b)(4) | "A disclosure of appropriate alternative procedures or courses of treatment, if any, that might be advantageous to the subject;" |
| (b)(5) | "A statement describing the extent, if any, to which confidentiality of records identifying the subject will be maintained;" |
| (b)(6) | "For research involving more than minimal risk, an explanation as to whether any compensation and an explanation as to whether any medical treatments are available if injury occurs and, if so, what they consist of, or where further information may be obtained;" |
| (b)(7) | "An explanation of whom to contact for answers to pertinent questions about the research and research subjects' rights, and whom to contact in the event of a research-related injury to the subject;" |
| (b)(8) | "A statement that participation is voluntary, refusal to participate will involve no penalty or loss of benefits to which the subject is otherwise entitled, and the subject may discontinue participation at any time without penalty or loss of benefits to which the subject is otherwise entitled; and" |
| (b)(9) | **NEW in 2018.** "One of the following statements about any research that involves the collection of identifiable private information or identifiable biospecimens: (i) A statement that identifiers might be removed from the identifiable private information or identifiable biospecimens and that, after such removal, the information or biospecimens could be used for future research studies or distributed to another investigator for future research studies without additional informed consent from the subject or the legally authorized representative, if this might be a possibility; or (ii) A statement that the subject's information or biospecimens collected as part of the research, even if identifiers are removed, will not be used or distributed for future research studies." |

### 2.3 Additional elements — 45 CFR 46.116(c)

When appropriate, one or more of the following must also be provided (verbatim):

| § | Verbatim text |
|---|---|
| (c)(1) | "A statement that the particular treatment or procedure may involve risks to the subject (or to the embryo or fetus, if the subject is or may become pregnant) that are currently unforeseeable;" |
| (c)(2) | "Anticipated circumstances under which the subject's participation may be terminated by the investigator without regard to the subject's or the legally authorized representative's consent;" |
| (c)(3) | "Any additional costs to the subject that may result from participation in the research;" |
| (c)(4) | "The consequences of a subject's decision to withdraw from the research and procedures for orderly termination of participation by the subject;" |
| (c)(5) | "A statement that significant new findings developed during the course of the research that may relate to the subject's willingness to continue participation will be provided to the subject;" |
| (c)(6) | "The approximate number of subjects involved in the study;" |
| (c)(7) | **NEW in 2018.** "A statement that the subject's biospecimens (even if identifiers are removed) may be used for commercial profit and whether the subject will or will not share in this commercial profit;" |
| (c)(8) | **NEW in 2018.** "A statement regarding whether clinically relevant research results, including individual research results, will be disclosed to subjects, and if so, under what conditions; and" |
| (c)(9) | **NEW in 2018.** "For research involving biospecimens, whether the research will (if known) or might include whole genome sequencing (i.e., sequencing of a human germline or somatic specimen with the intent to generate the genome or exome sequence of that specimen)." |

### 2.4 Other 2018 Revised Common Rule additions worth flagging for reviewers

- **§46.116(d)** — Broad consent for storage, maintenance, and secondary research use of identifiable private information or identifiable biospecimens (new alternative pathway in 2018).
- **§46.116(g)** — Screening, recruiting, or determining eligibility provisions (new in 2018; permits limited information collection without full consent under specified conditions).
- **§46.116(h)** — Posting of clinical-trial consent form on a public federal website within 60 days of last study visit (new in 2018; applies to federally-conducted or supported clinical trials).
- **§46.117(c)(2)** — Short-form consent must state "the key information required by §46.116(a)(5)(i) was presented first to the subject, before other information, if any, was provided" (cross-reference to the new key-information requirement).

## 3. Required elements — WHO ERC and CIOMS 2016 (woven together)

### 3.1 What the WHO ERC ICF template requires

The WHO ERC's clinical-studies ICF template is structured in two parts: **Part I — Information Sheet** and **Part II — Consent Certificate** (verbatim from the WHO ERC page). The template *page* itself (`who.int/groups/research-ethics-review-committee/guidelines-on-submitting-research-proposals-for-ethics-review/templates-for-informed-consent-forms`) does not enumerate the required content sections inline; it instructs investigators to download the .doc template, which sets out the required sections in a fillable structure. As the body text on the page is the only stable, dateless web reference, the verbatim instructions reproduced here are limited to those visible on the page itself:

- *"The informed consent form consists of two parts: the information sheet and the consent certificate."*
- *"The language used throughout form should be at the level of a local student of class 6th/8th."*
- *"square brackets indicate where specific information is to be inserted; bold lettering indicates sections or wording which should be included; standard lettering is used for explanations to researchers only and must not be included in your consent forms."*
- *"The logo of the Institution must be used on the ICF and not the WHO logo."*

The companion WHO PDF (`informed-decision-making.pdf`) adds verbatim: *"Once approved by the WHO ethics review committee (ERC), the consent forms become part of the project protocol. Forms should be written in the prospective participants' mother tongue, and when this is not English, an English language translation should be provided as well… Ideally, translated consent forms should be back-translated and compared with the original to ensure accuracy."*

**Verbatim section structure of the downloadable WHO ERC clinical-studies ICF template.** The full template was downloaded from `who.int/.../templates-for-informed-consent-forms` (file: `ethics-informedconsent-clinicalstudies.doc`) on 30 April 2026 and parsed locally. The template is structured exactly as follows (headings reproduced verbatim from the .doc file).

The template opens with a verbatim instruction to investigators: *"This template is for either clinical trials or clinical research"* and *"language used throughout form should be at the level of a local student of class 6th/8th"*. It also states verbatim: *"The informed consent form consists of two parts: the information sheet and the consent certificate."* and *"You will be given a copy of the full Informed Consent Form."*

**Part I: Information Sheet** — required section headings, in order:

1. **Introduction** — who you are, that the participant is being invited to participate, and that they may take time to reflect and ask questions.
2. **Purpose of the research** — explained in lay terms, with local/simplified terminology preferred.
3. **Type of Research Intervention** — brief statement (e.g., vaccine, interview, biopsy, finger pricks).
4. **Participant selection** — why this person has been chosen.
5. **Voluntary Participation** — explicit statement that participation is voluntary; alternative treatment if they decline.
6. **Information on the Trial Drug [Name of Drug]** — *included only if the protocol is for a clinical trial*. Must cover: trial phase, drug manufacturer, known experience, all known side-effects/toxicity.
7. **Procedures and Protocol**, with two subsections:
   - **A. Unfamiliar Procedures** — for clinical trials must explain randomization/blinding/placebo/rescue medicine; for clinical research must explain biopsy and anesthesia procedures and blood-sample volumes (preferably with a visual aid).
   - **B. Description of the Process** — step-by-step description of what will happen at each visit.
8. **Duration** — total time commitment including follow-up.
9. **Side Effects** — known/anticipated side effects and what will happen if they occur.
10. **Risks** — anticipated risks plus level of care available if harm occurs, who provides it, and who pays.
11. **Benefits** — actual benefits (not entitlements); divided into individual / community / societal benefit.
12. **Reimbursements** — reimbursement of expenses (travel, lost wages); WHO does *not* encourage incentives.
13. **Confidentiality** — how data will be kept confidential; awareness that participation may make participants more identifiable in their community.
14. **Sharing the Results** — plan for sharing results with participants and broader publication.
15. **Right to Refuse or Withdraw** — reconfirmation that participation is voluntary and may be withdrawn without affecting routine clinical care.
16. **Alternatives to Participating** — *included only if the study involves investigational drugs or new therapeutic procedures*; describes the established standard treatment.
17. **Who to Contact** — name and contact of someone informed and accessible; statement that the protocol has been reviewed and approved by the local IRB and the WHO ERC.

The template also includes, after most sections, **"Examples of question to elucidate understanding"** — brief comprehension-check questions an investigator should ask. These are illustrative, not mandatory verbatim, and should be adapted to the study.

**Part II: Certificate of Consent** — required components:

1. **Participant statement** (verbatim required wording, to be read or read-to the participant): *"I have read the foregoing information, or it has been read to me. I have had the opportunity to ask questions about it and any questions that I have asked have been answered to my satisfaction. I consent voluntarily to participate as a participant in this research."* — followed by printed name, signature, date.
2. **If illiterate** — verbatim required wording for an impartial literate witness (with no connection to the research team, ideally selected by the participant): *"I have witnessed the accurate reading of the consent form to the potential participant, and the individual has had the opportunity to ask questions. I confirm that the individual has given consent freely."* — followed by printed name and signature of witness, date, and a thumb-print of the participant.
3. **Statement by the researcher/person taking consent** — verbatim required wording: *"I have accurately read out the information sheet to the potential participant, and to the best of my ability made sure that the participant understands that the following will be done: [1, 2, 3]. I confirm that the participant was given an opportunity to ask questions about the study, and all the questions asked by the participant have been answered correctly and to the best of my ability. I confirm that the individual has not been coerced into giving consent, and the consent has been given freely and voluntarily. A copy of this ICF has been provided to the participant."* — followed by printed name and signature of researcher, date.

**Reviewer-relevant template directives** (verbatim, from the "Notes to Researchers" preamble):

- *"The logo of the Institution must be used on the ICF and not the WHO logo."*
- *"square brackets indicate where specific information is to be inserted"*; *"bold lettering indicates sections or wording which should be included"*; *"standard lettering is used for explanations to researchers only and must not be included in your consent forms."*

The companion WHO PDF (`informed-decision-making.pdf`) adds verbatim: *"Once approved by the WHO ethics review committee (ERC), the consent forms become part of the project protocol. Forms should be written in the prospective participants' mother tongue, and when this is not English, an English language translation should be provided as well… Ideally, translated consent forms should be back-translated and compared with the original to ensure accuracy."*

### 3.1A WHO ERC template variants for special populations and study types

The WHO ERC publishes six ICF template variants — the clinical-studies template covered in §3.1 above is the canonical one. The other five are for specific populations and study types. Each was downloaded from the WHO ERC templates page on 30 April 2026, parsed locally, and verbatim section structures are reproduced below. The actual `.doc` files are bundled in the skill's `assets/` folder so the reviewer can hand the appropriate template to a PI as a starting point. **All five variants share the same Part I + Part II two-part structure** (Information Sheet + Certificate) and the same illiterate-witness, thumb-print, and researcher-statement Part II procedures as the clinical-studies template. The variants below differ in which Part I sections apply.

> Source for all five variants: World Health Organization, Research Ethics Review Committee. *Templates for informed consent forms.* `https://www.who.int/groups/research-ethics-review-committee/guidelines-on-submitting-research-proposals-for-ethics-review/templates-for-informed-consent-forms`. Accessed 30 April 2026. Open access.

#### 3.1A.1 Paediatric assent — `informed-assent-for-children-minors.doc`

Verbatim preamble notes: *"An Informed Assent Form does not replace a consent form signed by parents or guardians. The assent is in addition to the consent and signals the child's willing cooperation in the study."* and *"language should be at a level appropriate to the child's age and development"* and *"This template is written for a pre-adolescent or young adolescent."* (i.e., the supplied template targets children aged ~12–16; younger children require simpler language adapted further by the investigator).

Part I (Information Sheet) — section headings as printed, each phrased as a child-facing question:

1. **Introduction**
2. **Purpose: "Why are you doing this research?"**
3. **Choice of participants: "Why are you asking me?"**
4. **Participation is voluntary: "Do I have to do this?"**
5. **Information on the Trial Drug [Name of Drug]** — *included only if the protocol is for a clinical trial.*
6. **Procedures: "What is going to happen to me?"**
7. **Risks: "Is this bad or dangerous for me?"**
8. **Discomforts: "Will it hurt?"**
9. **Benefits: "Is there anything good that happens to me?"**
10. **Confidentiality: "Is everybody going to know about this?"**
11. **Compensation: "What happens if I get hurt?"**
12. **Sharing the Findings: "Will you tell me the results?"**

Part II (Certificate of Assent):

- Child's statement: *"I agree to take part in the research."* — print name, signature, date.
- "Only if child assents:" — additional sub-statement clarifying the child's voluntary willingness, signed by the researcher in the child's presence.
- "If illiterate" — impartial-witness statement (literate, not part of the research team, selected by the participant), with thumb-print of the child.
- "Statement by the researcher/person taking consent" — verbatim required, parallel to the clinical-studies template.
- "Copy provided to the participant ________(initialed by researcher/assistant)" — explicit copy-handover sign-off, unique to this variant.

This assent must be paired with a parental/guardian consent form (clinical or qualitative variant per the study type). The reviewer should check both are present in the package.

#### 3.1A.2 Sample-storage / future-use consent — `consent-for-storage-and-future-use-of-unused-samples.doc`

Verbatim preamble note: *"Include the following section if the research protocol calls for storage and future use of samples"*. This is an **additional** consent layered on top of the primary study consent — its title is *"Additional Consent to [Name of Project]"* — to give participants the choice to allow storage and future use of their unused samples (blood, tissue, sperm, sputum, etc.). Verbatim: *"This Statement of Consent consists of two parts: Information Sheet (to share information about unused samples with you) [and] Certificate of Consent (to record your agreement)."*

Part I (Information Sheet) — content elements (the template uses descriptive paragraphs rather than numbered headings; required content per the verbatim instructions):

1. Explanation that the participant gave permission only for the *current* research, and that storage requires additional decision.
2. Possible future research uses (including genetic research where applicable), in lay terms; ability to opt out of specific kinds of future research.
3. The choice between **traceable** (sample remains linked to identifiers) and **de-identified** storage, with risks/benefits of each option, including the researchers' obligations to inform the participant of clinically relevant findings if the sample remains linked.
4. Statement that the sample will not be sold for profit and that any future research using the sample will be approved.
5. **Right to Refuse and Withdraw** — explicit section heading.
6. **Confidentiality** — explicit section heading.

Part II (Certificate of Consent) — multiple stacked statements:

- Primary statement of agreement to the Additional Consent.
- *"AND (if the sample is to be stored)"* — additional sub-statement giving permission for storage with linked vs. delinked option.
- *"AND"* — further stacked sub-statements where applicable (e.g., genetic research permission).
- Print name, signature, date — for each agreement stacked.
- "If illiterate" — impartial-witness statement with thumb-print.
- "Statement by the researcher/person taking consent" — parallel to the clinical-studies template.

Reviewer note: this template is **layered** — it is not a standalone replacement for the primary study ICF. If the package contains research using stored samples, both the primary ICF and this Additional Consent should be present. If the protocol mentions future use of samples but only the primary ICF is in the package, that is a finding.

#### 3.1A.3 Qualitative-studies consent — `informed-consent-for-qualitative-studies.doc`

Verbatim preamble: *"This template is for research interventions that use questionnaires, in-depth interviews or focus group discussions"*. Reading-level direction is the same as the clinical-studies template: *"language used throughout form should be at the level of a local student of class 6th/8th"*. Verbatim institutional-letterhead note: *"[YOUR INSTITUTIONAL LETTER HEAD] Please do not submit consent forms on the WHO letter head"*.

Part I (Information Sheet) — section headings:

1. **Introduction**
2. **Purpose of the research**
3. **Type of Research Intervention** — adapted to specify "questionnaires", "in-depth interviews", or "focus group discussions".
4. **Participant Selection**
5. **Voluntary Participation** — with an "OR" alternative phrasing for circumstances where the participant has an existing relationship with the institution.
6. **Procedures** — with sub-cases per intervention type: Example 1 (focus groups), Example 2 (interviews), Example 3 (questionnaire surveys).
7. **Duration**
8. **Risks**
9. **Benefits**
10. **Reimbursements**
11. **Confidentiality** — with a verbatim sub-section *"The following applies to focus groups:"* covering the limits of confidentiality in group settings (no participant can be guaranteed confidentiality from other group members).
12. **Sharing the Results**
13. **Right to Refuse or Withdraw**
14. **Who to Contact**

Part II (Certificate of Consent) — standard structure (statement, illiterate-witness with thumb-print, researcher statement). Note the unique focus-group risk that confidentiality cannot be guaranteed by the researcher because other group members may discuss what was said outside the group; the qualitative ICF should disclose this in the Confidentiality section.

#### 3.1A.4 Parental consent for qualitative research — `informed-parental-consent-for-research-involving-children-(qualitative).doc`

Verbatim preamble: *"For use with Participant Observation, Focus Group Discussions, Interviews, and Surveys"*. Reading-level direction same as clinical-studies. Title: *"Informed Consent Form for [name of group, e.g., parents of adolescent girls and boys participating in...]"*.

Part I (Information Sheet) — section headings:

1. **Introduction**
2. **Purpose**
3. **Type of Research Intervention**
4. **Selection of Participants**
5. **Voluntary Participation**
6. **Procedure** — with sub-cases: focus group discussions (1), interviews (2), questionnaire surveys (3).
7. **Duration**
8. **Risks and Discomforts**
9. **Benefits**
10. **Reimbursements**
11. **Confidentiality** — with the same focus-group sub-section as the qualitative-studies variant.
12. **Sharing of Research Findings**
13. **Right to refuse or withdraw**
14. **Who to Contact**

Part II (Certificate of Consent) — agreement is by the **parent/guardian on behalf of the child**: *"Print Name of Parent or Guardian"*, *"Signature of Parent of Guardian"*. Standard illiterate-witness procedure with thumb-print and researcher statement. **This consent must be paired with a paediatric assent form** (§3.1A.1) when the child is of an age and developmental level capable of assent.

#### 3.1A.5 Parental consent for clinical research with children — *not bundled in v1*

The WHO ERC page hosts six template variants, including a sixth variant *"parental consent for research involving children (clinical)"* — i.e., the clinical analogue of §3.1A.4 for clinical (non-qualitative) research with children. **This variant is not currently bundled** because the file was not retrieved when this reference was drafted. When the package contains clinical research with children, the reviewer should expect a parental consent that mirrors the structure of the clinical-studies template (§3.1) but with the agreement made by a parent/guardian on behalf of the child. The template should be downloaded from the WHO ERC page and added when next iterating on this skill.

### 3.2 What WHO 2011 SOG says about informed consent

WHO 2011 (Standards and Operational Guidance for Ethics Review of Health-Related Research with Human Participants; ISBN 9789241502948) addresses informed consent at multiple points. Verbatim relevant excerpts (Bookshelf mirror NBK310671):

- On the REC's role: *"Approval or disapproval is based on the ethical acceptability of the research, including its social value and scientific validity, an acceptable ratio of potential benefits to risks of harm, the minimization of risks, adequate informed consent procedures (including cultural appropriateness and mechanisms to ensure voluntariness), measures to ensure protection of vulnerable populations, fair procedures for selection of participants, and attention to the impact of research on the communities from which participants will be drawn, both during the research and after it is complete."*
- On consent's relationship to overall ethics: *"While informed consent to research is important, the fact that a participant or surrogate may be willing to consent to research does not, in itself, mean that the research is ethically acceptable."*
- On community engagement: *"Researchers should actively engage with communities in decision-making about the design and conduct of research (including the informed consent process), while being sensitive to and respecting the communities' cultural, traditional and religious practices."*
- On capacity and waivers: *"Competent individuals are entitled to choose freely whether to participate in research, and to make decisions based on an adequate understanding of what the research entails. Decisions for children or adults who lack the mental capacity to provide informed consent should be made by an authorized surrogate decision-maker. RECs should examine the process through which informed consent will occur, as well as the information that will be provided. RECs may waive the requirement of informed consent only when doing so is consistent with international guidelines and national standards."*

### 3.3 What CIOMS 2016 Guideline 9 and Guideline 10 say

CIOMS 2016 (DOI `10.56759/rgxl7405`) places informed consent in Guidelines 9 and 10. The introductory rule of Guideline 9 states verbatim: *"Researchers must not initiate research involving humans without obtaining each participant's individual informed consent or that of a legally authorized representative, unless researchers have received explicit approval to do so from a research ethics committee."*

Guideline 9 goes on to require that researchers (verbatim, condensed list as enumerated on Bookshelf NBK614414):
- *"refrain from unjustified deception or withholding of relevant information, undue influence, or coercion (see Guideline 10 – Modifications and waivers of informed consent);"*
- *"ensure that the potential participant has been given sufficient opportunity and time to consider whether to participate;"*
- (and additional duties on documentation: *"Documentation of consent. Consent may be indicated in a number of ways. The participant may express consent orally, or sign a consent form."*)

Guideline 10 verbatim sets the bar for any modification or waiver: *"A modification of informed consent involves making changes to the informed consent process, most frequently in relation to providing information and documenting the participant's informed consent. A waiver of consent allows researchers to conduct studies without obtaining fully informed consent."* The Guideline allows waiver only when (paraphrased structure of the guideline; verbatim conditions are listed in Bookshelf NBK614433): the research would not be feasible without the waiver; the research has important social value; the research poses no more than minimal risks; and arrangements are made to provide pertinent information to participants when feasible after participation.

CIOMS 2016 **Appendix 2** ("Obtaining Informed Consent: Essential Information for Prospective Research Participants") provides the canonical CIOMS list of disclosure elements; it functions as the CIOMS analogue of 45 CFR 46.116(b)/(c) but is broader. Reviewers should consult Appendix 2 in the CIOMS PDF directly when assessing whether the protocol's ICF content is adequate by international standards.

### 3.4 Where WHO/CIOMS exceeds or differs from the US Common Rule

| Topic | US Common Rule (§46.116) | WHO ERC + WHO 2011 + CIOMS 2016 |
|---|---|---|
| "Key Information" concise summary at start | Required (§46.116(a)(5)(i)) | Not framed in those words; WHO ERC template structure is broadly analogous via the Information Sheet; CIOMS Guideline 9 emphasises *understanding* but does not mandate a specific concise-summary block. |
| Reasonable-person standard | Required (§46.116(a)(4)) | Implicit in CIOMS Guideline 9's information-quality requirement; not stated as a discrete legal test. |
| Community/stakeholder consultation | Not required for individual consent | **Required** (WHO 2011 SOG; CIOMS Guideline 7 on community engagement; cross-referenced in Guideline 9). This is a key difference for community-based research in WHO/CIOMS-aligned ethics frameworks. |
| Translation and back-translation | Not required by §46.116; required by US sites' IRBs as policy | **Explicitly recommended** by the WHO ERC ICF guidance PDF and routinely required by CIOMS-aligned RECs. |
| Witness for orally consented illiterate participants | Permitted via short-form consent (§46.117(b)(2)) | Required in WHO ERC template (Consent Certificate has a thumb-print/witness section); CIOMS Guideline 9 commentary supports impartial-witness procedures. |
| Post-trial care | Not addressed in §46.116 | Addressed by Declaration of Helsinki 2024 and CIOMS Guideline 14; reviewers must check this is in the ICF. |
| Genomic/biospecimen disclosures | Required at §46.116(c)(7)–(9) | CIOMS Guideline 11 covers biological materials; Appendix 2 covers genomic disclosures in CIOMS context. |

### 3.5 Have WHO published a post-2011 update specifically on informed consent?

As of the verification date for this document (30 April 2026), the WHO ERC public page that hosts the ICF templates (URL in §1) is undated and presents the templates without a "version 2024" or "version 2025" label. WHO has published other research-ethics outputs since 2011 (notably the 2024 *Guidance for best practices for clinical trials*, ISBN 9789240097711, which SPIRIT 2025 cites as reference 38) but none of these supersedes the 2011 SOG specifically on informed consent. PAHO/WHO regional outputs (e.g., the July 2025 PAHOERC document *Questions to guide the ethics analysis of research involving humans*) are regional rather than global. **No WHO global standalone replacement of WHO 2011 SOG on informed consent has been identified as of 30 April 2026.** See Verification notes for the consequence.

### 3.6 Contradictions / tensions between WHO 2011 SOG and CIOMS 2016 — flagged

The user requested explicit flagging of contradictions rather than silent reconciliation. Two areas of *tension* (not direct contradiction) are visible in the primary texts:

1. **Threshold for waiver of consent.** WHO 2011 SOG says verbatim *"RECs may waive the requirement of informed consent only when doing so is consistent with international guidelines and national standards."* (NBK310671). It does not enumerate criteria. CIOMS 2016 Guideline 10 enumerates specific conditions (research not feasible otherwise, important social value, no more than minimal risk, post-hoc information where feasible). When the two are read together, WHO defers to CIOMS — but a literal reading of WHO 2011 alone could be read as more permissive (any "consistent with international guidelines" framework). Reviewers should apply the more specific CIOMS criteria.

2. **Community consent and individual consent.** WHO 2011 SOG places community engagement as a co-equal duty (*"Researchers should actively engage with communities…"*). CIOMS 2016 (Guideline 7) likewise mandates community engagement but is explicit that community engagement *does not replace* individual consent. WHO 2011 leaves the relationship more ambiguous. This is not a contradiction per se but is a frequent source of misinterpretation in LMIC protocols where investigators sometimes treat community-leader assent as sufficient. Apply CIOMS 2016 Guideline 7 as the controlling authority on this distinction.

## 4. Readability and comprehension best practice

### 4.1 Recommended reading-grade level — with citation

The primary source most directly applicable in WHO/CIOMS-aligned settings is the **WHO ERC template page** itself, which states verbatim: *"The language used throughout form should be at the level of a local student of class 6th/8th."* (Source: `who.int/groups/research-ethics-review-committee/guidelines-on-submitting-research-proposals-for-ethics-review/templates-for-informed-consent-forms`.) This is the WHO ERC's own number.

Two complementary US primary sources for comparison:

- **NIH OSP (May 2024) digital-health resource**, verbatim: *"The sample language in this resource was designed to ensure readability and comprehension (with an overall goal of not exceeding an 8th grade reading level) with language that conveys the necessary information to research participants."* (Source: NIH OSP, `osp.od.nih.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/DigitalHealthResource_Final.pdf`, p. 1.) This is a US-government primary source giving an 8th-grade target.
- **45 CFR §46.116(a)(3)** sets the legal standard as "language understandable to the subject or the legally authorized representative" — **no specific grade level is set in regulation**. Specific grade-level numbers (6th, 8th) are guidance, not regulatory requirements.

For LMIC and rural participant populations where literacy is variable, the WHO ERC's "class 6th/8th" range is the operative target. For US/EU-based research, the 8th-grade target from NIH OSP is widely cited as the comparable benchmark.

### 4.2 Comprehension-check methods

The WHO ERC template page states verbatim: *"These templates include examples of key questions that may be asked at the end of each section, that could ensure the understanding of the information being provided, especially if the research study is complex."* This is the WHO-endorsed approach: **teach-back / comprehension questions embedded after each section** of the Information Sheet. The .doc templates display these example questions in italic for the investigator to adapt.

### 4.3 Plain-language guidance

- Use second-person "you" rather than first-person "I" (a near-universal IRB convention; not codified in §46.116).
- Avoid lists of isolated facts: §46.116(a)(5)(ii) verbatim — *"must be organized and presented in a way that does not merely provide lists of isolated facts, but rather facilitates the prospective subject's or legally authorized representative's understanding of the reasons why one might or might not want to participate."*
- Use short sentences; replace technical terms with locally meaningful equivalents; provide a glossary.

### 4.4 Pictograms and translations

- **Translation and back-translation.** WHO companion PDF verbatim: *"Forms should be written in the prospective participants' mother tongue, and when this is not English, an English language translation should be provided as well… Ideally, translated consent forms should be back-translated and compared with the original to ensure accuracy."* The reviewer should confirm forward translation **and** independent back-translation, identify the working languages of the actual participant population (e.g., from the protocol's recruitment plan and study sites), and check that all required language versions are in the package.
- **Pictograms.** Neither the WHO ERC page nor 45 CFR 46.116 mandates pictograms. Their use is supported by general health-literacy literature but is not a primary-source requirement of any of the documents in §1. Reviewers may *recommend* pictograms in low-literacy settings but cannot *require* them on the basis of these primary sources.

### 4.5 Operational readability check — what the skill flags as a finding during the ICF pass

Readability is one of the most concrete checkable items in an ICF appraisal, and one of the most commonly under-addressed. The skill should systematically flag the following as findings during every ICF pass:

1. **Reading-grade-level finding.** The skill cannot directly compute a grade level on a PDF in most environments, but it can and should: (a) quote the relevant primary-source threshold (WHO ERC's *"class 6th/8th"* for LMIC settings, or the NIH OSP 8th-grade benchmark for US/EU settings); (b) qualitatively flag obvious indicators of high reading level — long sentences (>25 words), multi-clause complex sentences, technical vocabulary not explained in lay terms, undefined acronyms, Latinate medical terminology; (c) explicitly recommend the reviewer or PI run a readability tool before submission and list specific tools they can use:
   - **Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level** — built into Microsoft Word (File → Options → Proofing → "Show readability statistics") and many online tools.
   - **SMOG Index** — designed for health-literacy materials; available at `https://readabilityformulas.com/` and similar sites.
   - **Coleman-Liau Index** and **Automated Readability Index (ARI)** — also widely available.
   - **Hemingway Editor** (`hemingwayapp.com`) — interactive sentence-level feedback on complexity, passive voice, and grade level.
   - The Python library `textstat` if the reviewer has script-execution access.
2. **Sentence-length finding.** Flag any sentence in the ICF that exceeds 25 words. A 25-word ceiling is a common health-literacy heuristic; the legal standard at §46.116(a)(3) is just "language understandable to the subject", but long sentences are a recognised obstacle to that standard.
3. **Jargon finding.** Flag medical or technical terms that appear without a lay-language explanation. The standard is *understandability to the participant*, not formal correctness.
4. **Lists-of-isolated-facts finding.** Flag any section that reads as a bulleted dump of facts without orienting prose, citing 45 CFR §46.116(a)(5)(ii) verbatim.
5. **Comprehension-check finding.** Flag any ICF section in which the WHO ERC template's "Examples of question to elucidate understanding" pattern is absent and the study is complex enough to warrant teach-back. Cite WHO ERC template page verbatim.

Each finding should be recorded with verbatim source citations per the skill's source citation requirement.

### 4.6 Operational translation-completeness check — what the skill flags as a finding during the ICF pass

Translation requirements are concrete and checkable. The skill should:

1. **Identify the working languages.** Read the protocol's recruitment plan, study sites, and population description to identify which languages the participant population reads or speaks. Common cases: a single non-English working language; multiple languages across sites; primarily oral languages requiring oral-consent procedures (§5.2). Flag the identified language set explicitly.
2. **Check that all language versions are in the package.** For each working language identified, the skill flags whether the corresponding ICF translation is present. If the protocol mentions translation but no translated ICF is in the package, that is a Major-class fact (per WHO companion PDF and CIOMS Guideline 9).
3. **Check translation provenance.** Flag whether the package documents who performed the translation and back-translation, and whether the translators are independent professional translators. CIOMS-aligned RECs typically require this; the legal text in §46.116 does not, but it is a routinely-flagged operational requirement.
4. **Check reconciliation.** Flag whether a reconciliation log (showing discrepancies between original and back-translation, with resolutions) is in the package.

Cite the WHO companion PDF verbatim for each finding, and flag the absence of independent professional translation services as a finding requiring reviewer attention. Engaging professional translation is not a regulatory requirement under §46.116 but is a near-universal expectation in WHO/CIOMS-aligned ethics committees — say so in the finding.

## 5. LMIC and resource-limited setting considerations

### 5.1 Community engagement

Both WHO 2011 SOG and CIOMS 2016 Guideline 7 require community engagement. WHO 2011 verbatim: *"Researchers should actively engage with communities in decision-making about the design and conduct of research (including the informed consent process), while being sensitive to and respecting the communities' cultural, traditional and religious practices."* In rural and traditionally-organised communities this typically means engaging traditional authorities (e.g., chiefs, queen mothers, elders, councils) and local-government representatives at site initiation, plus pre-study sensitisation meetings. **Community engagement does not replace individual consent** (CIOMS 2016 Guideline 7 commentary). Community leaders may give *permission to approach* members of their community (verbatim, WHO ERC); they cannot give consent on their behalf.

### 5.2 Oral consent where literacy is limited

When a participant cannot read, the WHO ERC Consent Certificate template provides a standard structure:
- The information is read aloud in the participant's mother tongue;
- The participant gives verbal consent and applies a thumbprint instead of a signature;
- An **impartial witness** (literate, not part of the research team, chosen by the participant) signs the certificate to confirm that the information was read correctly and that consent was freely given.

This is consistent with **45 CFR §46.117(b)(2)** (US short-form procedure), which verbatim requires: *"A short form written informed consent form stating that the elements of informed consent required by §46.116 have been presented orally to the subject or the subject's legally authorized representative, and that the key information required by §46.116(a)(5)(i) was presented first to the subject, before other information, if any, was provided. The IRB shall approve a written summary of what is to be said to the subject or the legally authorized representative. When this method is used, there shall be a witness to the oral presentation."*

### 5.3 Translation and back-translation

Required by WHO ERC explanatory PDF (verbatim quoted in §4.4 above). Reviewer expectations:
- A named translator (with credentials) and a named back-translator (independent of the first).
- A reconciliation log showing discrepancies and resolutions.
- Submission of all language versions to the REC.

### 5.4 Impartial-witness procedures

WHO ERC template requires the witness to:
- Be literate;
- Not be a member of the research team;
- Be selected by the participant;
- Sign the consent certificate, confirming the information was read accurately and that consent was given freely.

### 5.5 Vulnerable populations

CIOMS 2016 dedicates Guidelines 15 (vulnerable persons), 16 (adults incapable of giving consent), 17 (children), 18 (women), 19 (pregnant and breastfeeding women), and 20 (research in disasters and disease outbreaks) to specific populations. Studies in maternal/child health and infectious disease commonly engage Guidelines 17, 18, and 19. Reviewers should:
- For children: confirm parental/guardian permission **plus** age-appropriate assent (CIOMS 17; WHO ERC has a separate assent template).
- For pregnant/breastfeeding women: confirm Guideline 19 conditions are addressed in the ICF (specific risks to fetus/infant, monitoring, post-pregnancy follow-up).
- For adults with impaired capacity: legally authorised representative + (where possible) participant assent, with documented capacity assessment.

### 5.6 Country- and region-specific requirements

CIOMS and WHO are international. Most countries have additional national-level ICF requirements administered by national research-ethics boards and (for clinical trials of medicinal products) national medicines regulatory authorities. The reviewer should establish the applicable national framework during Phase A and apply it alongside this reference. Examples of regional/national authorities include the **African Vaccine Regulatory Forum (AVAREF)** for vaccine trials with regional scope, **Ghana Health Service Ethics Review Committee (GHS-ERC)** and **Ghana Food and Drugs Authority (FDA)** in Ghana, **MHRA / HRA** in the UK, **EMA / national competent authorities** in EU member states, **OHRP / FDA / IRB** in the US, **CDSCO / Indian Council of Medical Research** in India, etc. National-framework reference files are out of scope for this draft — the reviewer is responsible for cross-checking against the applicable national framework.

## 7. Reviewer guidance — checklist

Verify each of the following against the protocol package and ICF:

- [ ] ICF starts with a concise Key Information section addressing why one might or might not want to participate (§46.116(a)(5)(i)).
- [ ] All 9 basic elements of §46.116(b) are present and accurate.
- [ ] Each appropriate additional element of §46.116(c) is present and accurate (especially (c)(7), (c)(8), (c)(9) for biospecimen/genomic studies; (c)(2) and (c)(4) for industry-sponsored studies; (c)(3) for studies with potential out-of-pocket costs).
- [ ] Reading level meets WHO ERC's "class 6th/8th" target; reviewer has run a Flesch–Kincaid or SMOG check on the English version.
- [ ] Local-language version (Twi/Bono dialect, as applicable) submitted, with documented forward and independent back-translation.
- [ ] Comprehension-check questions embedded after each Information Sheet section (per WHO ERC).
- [ ] Voluntariness statement (§46.116(b)(8)) is unambiguous; consent-taking setting minimises coercion and undue influence (§46.116(a)(2); CIOMS Guideline 9).
- [ ] No exculpatory language anywhere in the form (§46.116(a)(6)).
- [ ] Confidentiality statement (§46.116(b)(5)) is realistic and consistent with the protocol's data-management plan (SPIRIT 2025 Item 33).
- [ ] Compensation and treatment for research-related injury described (§46.116(b)(6); CIOMS Guideline 14).
- [ ] Post-trial care / ancillary care provisions described (CIOMS Guideline 14; aligned with SPIRIT 2025 Item 34).
- [ ] Impartial-witness procedure described for any participant who cannot read (WHO ERC template; §46.117(b)(2)).
- [ ] Community engagement plan referenced and consistent with the ICF process (WHO 2011 SOG; CIOMS 2016 Guideline 7).
- [ ] Vulnerable-population-specific protections in place (CIOMS Guidelines 15–20; especially 17 for children, 18 for women, 19 for pregnant/breastfeeding women, where applicable to the study).
- [ ] If consent waiver or modification requested: criteria of CIOMS Guideline 10 explicitly addressed in the protocol.
- [ ] If broad consent for biospecimen storage: §46.116(d) elements present.
- [ ] If a clinical trial under federal funding: §46.116(h) public posting plan in place.
- [ ] Contact persons for research questions (§46.116(b)(7)) include both a study-team contact and an REC contact (rights/complaints).
