## 2. Summary

SPIRIT (Standard Protocol Items: Recommendations for Interventional Trials) is an evidence-based reporting guideline for the *protocol* of a randomised trial (distinct from CONSORT, which targets the report of a completed trial). SPIRIT 2025 supersedes SPIRIT 2013; the BMJ paper states verbatim that *"The SPIRIT 2025 statement supersedes the SPIRIT 2013 statement, which should no longer be used or cited."*

The 2025 update was developed jointly by the merged SPIRIT–CONSORT executive group through (a) a 2013–2024 scoping review, (b) the SPIRIT-CONSORT Evidence Bibliographic database, (c) integration of recommendations from CONSORT Harms 2022, SPIRIT-Outcomes 2022, CONSORT NPT, and TIDieR, (d) a three-round Delphi survey (n = 317), and (e) a March 2023 consensus meeting (n = 30). The result is a **34-item** checklist plus a schedule diagram (the SPIRIT figure for enrolment, interventions, and assessments) and an Expanded Checklist with bullet-level guidance per item. The main structural changes versus SPIRIT 2013 are: a new **Open Science** section consolidating registration, protocol/SAP access, individual participant data sharing, funding/COI, and dissemination; new items on **patient and public involvement (PPI)** and **trial monitoring**; strengthened **harms** assessment; integration of **TIDieR** elements into the intervention description; and harmonisation of wording with CONSORT 2025.

## 4. What's new in 2025 vs 2013

The authoritative source for differences is **Box 1 of the BMJ paper** ("Summary of main changes in SPIRIT 2025"). Verbatim contents reproduced below, grouped by change type, with annotations on the three areas the user asked us to highlight: open science, harms, PPI.

### 4.1 New checklist items (n = 2)

- **Item 11 (PPI) — NEW.** Box 1: *"added item on how patients and the public are involved in the design, conduct, and reporting of the trial."* This is the new patient and public involvement requirement.
- **Item 29 (Trial monitoring) — NEW.** Box 1: *"added item on trial monitoring (replaces prior item on auditing)."*

### 4.2 Revised content (n = 5)

- **Item 4** — Box 1: *"revised item to include date of trial registration."*
- **Item 5** — Box 1: *"revised item to include where the statistical analysis plan can be accessed in addition to the trial protocol (previously covered under item on statistical methods)."*
- **Item 7b** — Box 1: *"revised item to include financial and other conflicts of interest of steering committee members."*
- **Items 24a/24b** — Box 1: *"split item into separate sub-items covering (a) who will be blinded and (b) how, and revised to include description of the similarity of interventions."*
- **Item 27d** — Box 1: *"revised to refer to sensitivity analyses."*

### 4.3 Deletions and mergers

- 2013 item on **auditing** — deleted (replaced by item 29 on trial monitoring).
- 2013 appendix item on **informed consent materials** — deleted as a separate item (informed consent itself remains as items 32a/32b).
- 2013 appendix item on **biological specimens** — deleted as a separate item.
- 2013 item on **access to data for trial investigators** — merged with data management (item 26).
- 2013 item on **authorship eligibility guidelines and use of professional writers** — merged with dissemination policy (item 8).

### 4.4 Open science (highlight requested)

The BMJ paper introduces an entirely new **Open Science** section (containing items 4, 5, 6, 7a, 7b, 8). Box 1 verbatim: *"Created a new section on open science that includes trial registration (item 4), access to the trial protocol and statistical analysis plan (item 5), plans for sharing de-identified participant level data (item 6), funding and conflicts of interest (item 7), and plans for dissemination of trial results (item 8)."*

### 4.5 Harms (highlight requested)

Harms reporting is materially expanded. Box 1 verbatim: *"Revised items to emphasise reporting of harms (items 10, 17, 27a)"* (citing CONSORT Harms 2022). In practice this means:
- Item 10 now requires *"Specific objectives related to benefits and harms"* (i.e. harms appear in the objectives, not only later).
- Item 17 now standalone for harms.
- Item 27a includes harms in statistical comparison: *"Statistical methods used to compare groups for primary and secondary outcomes, including harms"* — note the appended "including harms" phrase.
- Item 9a now requires summary of *"benefits and harms for each intervention"* in the background.

### 4.6 PPI (highlight requested)

Item 11 is new and was developed with PPI co-design (the SPIRIT–CONSORT 2025 Delphi process included 17 self-identified patient/public representatives, and three patient/public representatives sat on the consensus meeting per the BMJ "Patient and public involvement" disclosure section).

## 5. Integration with TIDieR

TIDieR (Hoffmann TC, et al. BMJ 2014;348:g1687, DOI `10.1136/bmj.g1687`; 12 items) is integrated rather than treated as a separate extension.

The BMJ paper states verbatim: *"Revised items to emphasise reporting of harms (items 10, 17, 27a) and to call for additional details relating to interventions and comparators (item 15a)."* The reference for the latter clause is TIDieR (citation 27 in the BMJ paper). Concretely:

| TIDieR concept (per Hoffmann 2014) | Where it appears in SPIRIT 2025 |
|---|---|
| Brief name; rationale/theory; what (materials, procedures); who provided; how (mode of delivery); where; when and how much; tailoring; modifications; how well (planned, actual) | Largely consolidated into **Item 15a**: *"Intervention and comparator with sufficient details to allow replication including how, when, and by whom they will be administered. If relevant, where additional materials describing the intervention and comparator (eg, intervention manual) can be accessed."* |
| Modifications during delivery (TIDieR item 9) | Reflected in **Item 15b** (criteria for discontinuing/modifying intervention) |
| Adherence/fidelity (TIDieR items 11–12) | Reflected in **Item 15c** (strategies/monitoring of adherence) |

The BMJ paper does **not** publish a one-to-one TIDieR-to-SPIRIT-2025 crosswalk. The mapping above is therefore inferred from textual cues in Box 1 and Item 15a; for any item-by-item TIDieR mapping, reviewers should consult the **SPIRIT 2025 Explanation and Elaboration** (BMJ 2025;389:e081660) and the SPIRIT 2025 Expanded Checklist (Appendix 1 of the BMJ paper).

## 6. Reviewer guidance — common deficiencies by section

Drawn from the published rationale in Box 1 and the body of the BMJ paper (which cites Gryaznov 2022, Speich 2022, Kasenda 2016, and other meta-research as evidence of common protocol gaps). These are written as cues for both the AI assistant applying the checklist and the human reviewer.

**Administrative information (1–3).** Watch for protocols missing a version date/identifier (item 2), undeclared sponsor authority over analysis or publication (item 3c), and missing endpoint adjudication or steering committee composition (item 3d).

**Open science (4–8).** Most frequent gaps: trial registry record incomplete or post-dated (item 4); statistical analysis plan referenced but not retrievable (item 5); IPD-sharing plans absent or "to be decided" (item 6); steering committee COIs not disclosed alongside PI COIs (item 7b, newly required); no dissemination-to-participants plan (item 8).

**Introduction (9–10).** Backgrounds that summarise only benefits (not harms) of each intervention now violate item 9a. Comparator justification (item 9b) is often missing in active-control trials. Objectives stated only in benefit terms violate item 10.

**PPI and trial design (11–12).** Item 11 is new — many legacy protocols will have *no* PPI section. Reviewers should require concrete plans (who, when, what role), not boilerplate. Item 12 frequently omits framework (superiority/non-inferiority/equivalence).

**Participants, interventions, outcomes (13–20).** Eligibility criteria for sites and intervention deliverers (item 14b) often omitted in surgical/behavioural trials. Item 15a TIDieR-level detail is the most common shortfall. Harms definition (item 17) and adherence-monitoring procedures (item 15c) are routinely under-specified. Sample-size assumptions (item 19) are commonly incomplete (effect size cited without variance or attrition).

**Assignment (21–24).** Block size disclosed in the main protocol body (violating item 21b's privacy provision) is a recurrent finding. Allocation concealment mechanism (item 22) is often vague. Item 24b "similarity of interventions" — newly added in 2025 — is rarely addressed in legacy protocols.

**Data collection, management, analysis (25–27).** Item 27c (handling of missing data) and item 27d (sensitivity analyses, newly emphasised) are commonly missing or deferred to "the SAP."

**Monitoring (28–29).** DMC charter location and independence statement (item 28a) often unclear; item 29 (replacing auditing) is new and many protocols lack any explicit trial-monitoring plan.

**Ethics (30–34).** Item 32b (ancillary-study consent for data/specimens) is the most under-addressed in LMIC protocols. Item 34 (ancillary and post-trial care, plus harm compensation) is consistent with the 2024 Declaration of Helsinki but is often omitted or limited to a one-line "as per local SOP."
